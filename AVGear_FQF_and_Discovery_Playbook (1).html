AVGear — Fast Qualification Framework & Discovery Call Playbook

Two tools in one: qualification gates for every inbound lead, and a live call reference for the discovery conversation.

Apply these four gates in sequence on every inbound inquiry — before scheduling a call or creating a deal. Each gate has a clear pass, flag, or fail outcome. A single fail ends the process immediately. A flag requires a documented HubSpot note and may require Director escalation before any response is sent.

Note & proceed
▾

4
Price expectation reality check
Address before advancing
▾
"Do you have a sense of what you're hoping to get for the equipment?"
Pass
No stated expectation, OR stated expectation is reasonably aligned with used market values. Prospect appears open to the valuation process.
Advance to deal creation and schedule discovery call.

Decline gracefully. Thank them, explain the model briefly, leave the door open. Log in HubSpot.

Pre-call preparation
Done before the call. A prepared SA runs a better call and builds credibility from the first sentence. Never go in cold.
Before the call
SA solo — standard call
Common categories, smaller lots
Standalone items in common pro AV categories (consoles, projectors, lights, speakers, amps, mics, wireless). Small to mid-size lots. Likely consignment or direct purchase structure with no complex logistics.
SA + Scott — complex call
Complex categories or large integrated systems
Integrated or racked systems, touring/production rigs, broadcast suites, full venue buildouts, 50+ items across multiple categories, or anything pointing toward asset disposition or a hybrid deal structure.
Pre-call checklist
  • Review the original inquiry and all HubSpot notes from qualification
  • Look up the prospect's company — website, LinkedIn, industry context
  • Determine call tier: SA solo or SA + Scott using the category guide above
  • If SA + Scott: brief Scott in advance — prospect name, company, rough equipment description, any qualification flags
  • Open HubSpot discovery call template — capture notes in real time during the call
  • Check for prior client history — any past deals, prior relationship context
  • Review any flags from qualification (price expectations, authority, volume concerns)
Opening & agenda setting
Establish rapport, set the call agenda, and position AVGear as a consultative partner — not a buyer pressuring them to sell.
2–3 min
Tone sets everything. The prospect should feel like this is a conversation where someone is genuinely trying to understand their situation — not a pitch call. Be warm, be curious, don't rush.
Opening framework
"Thanks for taking the time today — I'd love to start by learning more about your situation before we talk through how we work."
▾
Sets the consultative tone immediately. Signals you're here to listen, not pitch. Use this or a natural variation — don't read it verbatim. The goal is to make the prospect feel like the next 30 minutes is about them, not about closing a deal.
"I'll give us about 30–35 minutes today — I want to spend most of it understanding what you have and what you're trying to accomplish, then I'll walk you through our process and what options might make sense for your situation."
▾
Sets expectations for the call structure. Prospects are more comfortable when they know the agenda. It also signals you won't be pitching them for 30 minutes straight.
"Before I ask you anything — was there anything specific you were hoping to learn from this call?"
▾
Optional but powerful — gives the prospect space to surface what's on their mind. Sometimes they'll tell you exactly what they need to hear, which helps you tailor the rest of the call. Especially good for higher-value prospects or anyone who seemed notably eager to connect.
The "Why" — motivation
Understand why they're selling before asking what they're selling. The motivation shapes the entire deal structure recommendation.
5–7 min
This phase comes before the inventory discussion intentionally. A client who needs cash next week gets a completely different recommendation than one clearing storage with no urgency. Ask the why first. Then ask the what.
Questions — motivation
"Can you tell me what's prompting you to sell this equipment right now?"
▾
Open-ended opener — let them talk. Don't interrupt. The answer often contains the single most important piece of information on the call. Listen for the emotional driver (urgency, frustration, excitement about new gear) as much as the factual one.
"Has this equipment been in active regular use, or has it been sitting unused for some time?"
▾
Tells you about condition likelihood and urgency psychology. Equipment pulled from active service last month comes with different seller expectations than gear sitting in a storage room for three years. Unused gear often means lower urgency and a prospect who may not be fully sure they want to sell.
"Is there a specific reason for the timing — are you working against any kind of deadline?"
▾
Surfaces urgency directly. A lease ending, a facility closing, a fiscal year budget deadline — these change the deal type recommendation entirely. If they say there's no deadline, that's equally important — it confirms consignment is a viable path.
"Is this primarily about recouping capital for something specific, or more about clearing out the space and getting it off your hands?"
▾
Directly surfaces the financial vs. logistical motivation. Financial + urgency = direct purchase. Financial + no urgency = consignment. Logistical (just want it gone) = consignment often wins because it requires zero ongoing effort from the client.
"Are you in the middle of upgrading this equipment, or is this more of a standalone liquidation?"
▾
Surfaces the trade-in and hybrid opportunity. If they're upgrading, the conversation shifts — AVGear can potentially facilitate a trade against new equipment, or coordinate the sale timing with their purchase cycle. This is often the highest-satisfaction deal type for upgrade clients.
"Have you done anything like this before — sold professional AV equipment through a third party?"
▾
Gauges sophistication. An experienced seller understands used market pricing, consignment models, and processing timelines. A first-timer may need more education and may have unrealistic expectations that need to be addressed here rather than at valuation.
Motivation detector — click what you're hearing
As the prospect talks, click the motivation type that best matches what you're hearing. The recommended deal type and pitch approach will surface below — and carry into Phase 6.
Space constraint
"We need to clear the room" / storage filling up / facility reconfiguration
Capital need / urgency
"We need the money" / budget pressure / specific financial deadline
Upgrading equipment
"We're getting new gear" / transitioning to newer technology
Facility closeout
"We're shutting down" / department closing / full liquidation
End of life / unused
"It's been sitting in a corner for years" / gear at end of useful life
Flexible / exploring
"Just seeing what options are out there" / early research stage
Red flags to watch for in this phase
Prospect is shopping across multiple buyers simultaneously — indicates price sensitivity and lower commitment. Note it; don't disqualify, but set urgency expectations accordingly.
A specific high number they "need" to get — investigate whether it's based on market research or an arbitrary figure. Address gently here rather than letting it surface as a surprise at valuation.
A third party is driving the sale (estate, board, employer) and the contact has no personal stake — motivation and urgency may be unclear, and the decision process will be slower and more complex.
The "What" — inventory
Build a clear picture of scope, category, condition, and any known issues so Scott can conduct a properly contextualized valuation.
5–7 min
You don't need a full itemized list here — that comes after the call. The goal is scope and complexity enough to confirm category fit, size the valuation effort, and surface any condition or logistics flags before the list is submitted.
Questions — inventory scope
"Can you walk me through what you have — even just the major items or main categories?"
▾
Open-ended overview. Let them describe it in their own terms. Listen for: category alignment with professional AV, rough quantity, high-value anchor items (Yamaha CL5, Christie projectors, Robe or Martin fixtures), and complexity flags like integrated systems or items still installed and wired.
"Do you know any of the brands and models off the top of your head?"
▾
Brand and model info gives Scott the ability to build a rough valuation estimate before receiving the full list. It also tells you the tier of equipment — Shure vs. a no-name brand, Barco vs. a budget projector. Professional brands in common categories are typically faster to value and easier to sell.
"What would you say the overall condition is — has it been well-maintained and serviced regularly?"
▾
Condition is a major value driver. You're not grading it here — that happens during processing — but a rough sense of whether this is well-maintained touring gear or equipment that's been through hard use matters for the valuation. Also listen for any disclosures of known functional issues.
"Roughly how old is it — do you know when it was purchased or put into service?"
▾
Age helps contextualize condition claims and informs valuation expectations. Ten-year-old equipment that "works great" is still ten-year-old equipment in the used market. Also relevant for obsolescence — video technology ages and depreciates faster than quality audio gear.
"Is everything fully functional, or are there items with known issues?"
▾
Better to know now than to discover it during processing. Early disclosure lets you set expectations that items with functional problems affect pricing. Don't press hard — some sellers genuinely don't know the full status of everything they have.
"Do you have original packaging, flight cases, or accessories for any of it?"
▾
Cases, accessories, and original packaging meaningfully increase resale value. A moving head with its original road case is worth noticeably more than one without. Wireless systems with all antennas, power supplies, and original packaging are faster to sell and more valuable.
"Is the equipment still installed or racked, or has it already been broken down?"
▾
Critical for logistics planning. Equipment still installed in a rack, flown in a ceiling, or wired into a facility requires deinstallation — this affects timeline, cost, and potentially condition. Flag anything still installed to Scott and Sidnie during post-call briefing.
Goals & timeline
Understand what success looks like and whether timing constraints affect which deal structures are actually viable.
4–5 min
Questions — goals
"What does a good outcome look like for you from this process?"
▾
Intentionally open-ended. Some prospects say "get the most money possible." Others say "get it resolved as quickly and painlessly as possible." Others say both. The answer tells you what to lead with in Phase 6 and what trade-offs they'll be willing to make during deal structuring.
"Are you prioritizing maximizing what you get for it, or is speed and simplicity more important?"
▾
The clearest way to surface the maximize vs. liquidate tension. Most prospects land somewhere in the middle, which often makes consignment with a direct purchase fallback the right structure. If they say "definitely speed" → direct purchase. If they say "definitely maximize" → consignment.
"Do you have a number in mind — a target for what you'd like to walk away with?"
▾
If they have a specific number, Scott needs to know it before the valuation. If the number is realistic, great. If it's significantly above market, this is the moment to gently set expectations — not after the valuation. Don't promise anything in response; note it and say you'll factor it into the valuation conversation.
"Is there a deadline you're working toward — for space, financial, or any other reason?"
▾
Hard deadlines are the single biggest driver of deal type. A hard deadline may make consignment unviable if the timeline is too short for full market sell-through. In those cases, direct purchase or a hybrid with a buyout floor may be the only workable structure. Flag any hard deadline to Scott immediately post-call.
"How flexible are you on timing if it means a meaningfully better financial outcome?"
▾
Tests the real flexibility behind a stated deadline. Many prospects say they have a deadline but are actually flexible if the money is right. If they confirm the deadline is firm, stop trying to sell them on consignment — it sets up a bad client experience if the timeline can't be met.
Logistics & decision process
Establish the practical mechanics of getting inventory to AVGear, and confirm who needs to be involved before a deal can move forward.
3–5 min
Questions — logistics
"Where is the equipment located?"
▾
Simple but essential. State and city tells you whether this is a local pickup, regional freight, or cross-country shipment — all with different cost and timeline implications. For very distant locations, flag to Scott and Sidnie before making any logistics commitments.
"Do you have the ability to palletize and ship to us, or would you need AVGear to arrange pickup?"
▾
Client-managed shipping vs. AVGear-arranged pickup are two different logistics paths with different costs and timelines. Client-shipped is simpler and lower cost. AVGear pickup requires scheduling, labor, and freight coordination — needs to be properly scoped before it's promised.
"Is there anything about the location or access we should know — loading dock, elevator access, restricted hours?"
▾
Facility access issues can significantly complicate and delay pickup. Better to know now than to discover them during a scheduled pickup. Flag anything unusual to Sidnie during your post-call briefing.
Questions — decision process
"Besides yourself, is there anyone else who will need to be involved before you can move forward?"
▾
Revisits authority if it wasn't fully resolved at qualification. Some prospects say they're the decision maker but reveal an approver later. Surface this now — not after a valuation and proposal have been prepared.
"Are you talking to other companies about this, or is this your first conversation?"
▾
Tells you the competitive context. If they're shopping around, speed of valuation becomes a competitive advantage. It also tells you how much education is needed — a prospect on their third discovery call already understands the used market. Note it either way in HubSpot.
Service offering presentation
Present AVGear's services tailored to what you learned. Lead with the right fit — don't present all five deal types as equal options.
5–7 min
Complete Phase 2 first — select a motivation type in The Why phase to see the tailored deal type recommendation here.
Always cover — on every call, regardless of deal type
AVGear core value proposition — hit all five points on every call
Professional processing: Every item is individually tested using category-specific protocols, cosmetically graded on a four-tier scale, photographed, and assigned a unique SKU. The client receives a full condition report before anything is listed — no surprises at settlement.
Multi-channel reach: Items are sold across B2B direct outreach, Shopify, eBay, and live auctions — maximizing exposure and sell-through velocity rather than relying on a single channel.
Transparent reporting: The client receives regular updates throughout the selling process — what's sold, what's pending, and what the proceeds look like in real time. No black hole.
Clean settlement: A detailed settlement report at the close of the process with prompt payment. Nothing is ambiguous about the final proceeds calculation.
Dedicated client contact: From the moment inventory arrives, the client has a named Director of Client Success who owns their account — one person to call, one person who knows their deal inside and out.
Next steps & close
Summarize what you heard, explain the valuation process clearly, and leave with a confirmed next action for both parties.
2–3 min
Never end a discovery call without a confirmed next step. "I'll send you some information" is not a next step. The call should end with: a list being sent, and a follow-up call either scheduled or tied to a specific trigger.
Closing framework
Summarize what you heard: "Based on everything you've shared, [deal type] would probably be the best fit — [one sentence tied directly to their motivation]."
▾
Example: "Based on everything you've shared, consignment would probably be the best fit — you don't have a hard deadline, and you'd like to maximize what you get for the equipment rather than take a quick offer." Mirror their own words back. It demonstrates you were listening and builds confidence in the recommendation.
"The next step is to have our VP of Asset Strategy review your list and come back with a full valuation — that typically takes 24 to 48 hours once we have the list."
▾
Sets the SLA expectation clearly. Prospects who know the timeline don't feel ghosted during the valuation period. If they have a tight deadline, acknowledge it: "Given your timeline, we'll prioritize getting this back to you as quickly as possible."
"Can you send over a list after our call? Even a rough spreadsheet, a forwarded quote, or photos of labels works — whatever's easiest."
▾
Lower the barrier. Don't ask for a formatted spreadsheet — ask for whatever they have. A photo of a whiteboard works. The goal is to get something in Scott's hands so the valuation can start. You can always request more detail afterward.
"Would you be open to getting the follow-up call on the calendar now, or would you prefer I reach out once we have the valuation ready?"
▾
Always try to schedule the follow-up call before hanging up. A scheduled call is worth three times a "I'll reach out when it's ready" promise. If they prefer to wait, create the follow-up task in HubSpot immediately: "I'll reach out as soon as we have the valuation — likely within 48 hours."
